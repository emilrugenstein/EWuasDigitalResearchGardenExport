G6 Enkeshi

Overview

  • Name: Enkeshi
  • Player size: 2-6
  • Age: 30+
  • Sex: male
  • Duration: 30-90
  • Occasion: mental competition
  • Category: board game
  • Traditional: yes
  • Material needed: 1 Enkeshi board (with 2 times 8 holes) and 48 stones
  • Mechanics: pick-up and deliver, deck/bag building, area control, grid/area movement, pattern building

Background info

  • Name explanation: "Enkeshi (pl. Inkeshi)" comes from the Maa word "Enkesha" which means to compete. The stones are called "Inkishu" (meaning cows), possibly the name could also come from that.
  • History:
    • This is a very traditional game, which is for men only, but the story goes that a widowed woman introduced this game first to the Maasai men. It is said that the widow still had many cows, but no one to assist her with the cow deliveries of new calves. Because of that she taught this game to men so that they stay at her home. When the cows gave birth she could ask the playing men who were around to assist.
    • Traditionally this game is only for Maasai men after they underwent their last big ceremony, the "Olngesher". In this ceremony the left and right hand groups are joined together. The left hand group is a bit younger and has to "catch up" to the right hand group. See more here: Describtion > Maasai age groups While it was thus only allowed for the 5th and last age group to play the game, younger men would sometimes still build and play it in the sand while herding cattle.
    • The board is traditionally made from a special tree called Olng'osua by proper carving men.
  • Usage: Nowadays it is played less and less and only some people still know how to play it. But it used to be very famous before. The game has two sides with 1:1 as a default, but it is often played in teams as well (e.g. 2:2 or 3:3).
  • Information retrieval:
    • Ezekiel Roimen interviewed Lapiyioi ole Mampurre on the 13-11-2024

Instructions

  • The board has 8 holes on each sided and the game is played in two opposing teams.
  • For the beginning each side puts 4 stones in each of the 6 holes on the right hand and the two left holes are left empty.
  • One side starts by grabbing all the 4 stones from one hole on their side and continues anti-clockwise by putting the stones from the hand back into the wholes one each until the last stone is from the hand is put
  • If in that hole there are other stones, all stones from that hole are grapped and the previous step is repeated.
  • This is continued until a hole with no stones is reached with a last stone from the hand.
  • The first round of this is especially important as the first end points determine the bank of each side. These two stones in the holes were only one stone remains are called "Olker" (meaning male sheep) and they become the bank of the corresponding side. The bank is called "Emoti" (meaning pot).
  • Afterwards the players continue in the same manner but now they can start from any hole - expect the bank hole of the other side
  • The game ends when no movements can be done anymore. This happens when no other hole, besides the two bank holes, has enough stones to reach the next hole.
  • Now, the stones in the two banks are counted and the side with the most stones wins.
  • The rules of this game may seem simple, but the dynamics and strategies can quickly become sophisticated.
